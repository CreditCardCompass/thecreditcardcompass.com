Why No Annual Fee Cards Matter

Premium travel cards get most of the attention, but no annual fee cards play a crucial role in a smart rewards strategy. They serve two purposes: as a standalone card for someone just starting with travel rewards, and as a "catch-all" companion to a premium card that earns more on everyday purchases where the premium card only gets 1x.

The best no annual fee travel cards have quietly gotten very competitive. The Wells Fargo Autograph earns 3x on six major spending categories with zero fee — a rewards rate that would cost $95+ on most other cards.

The math on no-fee cards: A card earning 1.5x on all purchases with no fee beats a card earning 2x with a $95 annual fee unless you spend more than $9,500 per year on the card. For moderate spenders, no-fee cards often win.

Should You Get a No-Fee Card or a Premium Card?

The honest answer depends on how much you spend and how much you travel. Here's a quick way to think about it:

  • Fly 1-2 times per year: A no annual fee card is almost certainly the right call. You won't use enough travel perks to justify a $400+ annual fee.
  • Fly 3-5 times per year: A mid-tier card like the Chase Sapphire Preferred ($95) or Citi Strata Premier ($95) starts making sense — the sign-up bonuses alone often cover years of fees.
  • Fly 6+ times per year: Premium cards ($395-$695) typically pay for themselves through lounge access, travel credits, and superior rewards rates.

The best strategy for most people: One no-fee card for everyday purchases, one mid-tier travel card for the bonus categories and transfer partners. Total annual cost: $95. Total value: several hundred dollars in rewards.
