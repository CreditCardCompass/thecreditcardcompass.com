Chase Sapphire Reserve vs. Amex Platinum: Which Premium Card Actually Wins?

⏱ 10 min read✍️ The Credit Card Compass🔄 Last verified April 2026
The quick answer: If you want the best airport lounge experience and fly internationally often, the Amex Platinum wins on prestige and lounge quality. If you want a more practical premium card with stronger everyday rewards and a simpler annual credit, the Chase Sapphire Reserve is the better all-around card for most people. Read on for the full breakdown.

The Big Picture

These are the two most talked-about premium travel cards in the US — and for good reason. Both charge steep annual fees, both deliver serious travel perks, and both have passionate advocates. But they're designed for different types of travelers, and choosing the wrong one means paying hundreds of dollars a year for benefits you won't use.

The Chase Sapphire Reserve costs $550 per year. The Amex Platinum costs $695. That $145 gap sounds small, but over five years it adds up to $725 — so getting this decision right matters.

Here's the honest breakdown.

Annual Fees and Credits

Both cards have high annual fees that are partially offset by annual credits — but how those credits work is very different.

Chase Sapphire Reserve — $550

The Reserve's $300 travel credit is the cleanest benefit in premium cards. It applies automatically to the first $300 in travel purchases each year — airlines, hotels, Uber, rental cars, parking, tolls. No enrollment, no specific merchant, no expiration. You'll use it without thinking about it.

After that credit, your effective annual fee is $250. Add in the Global Entry credit ($25/year amortized) and you're at roughly $225 effective cost per year.

Amex Platinum — $695

The Platinum has more credits — but they require more management. You get a $200 airline fee credit (incidental fees only, not airfare), a $200 hotel credit through Amex Fine Hotels & Resorts, up to $240 in digital entertainment credits ($20/month), a $155 Walmart+ credit, and more.

On paper the credits total well over $695. In practice, many cardholders don't use all of them — particularly the Walmart+ credit and the digital entertainment credits which require monthly attention. If you maximize everything, the Platinum delivers exceptional value. If you don't, you're overpaying.

Winner — Annual Fee Simplicity
Chase Sapphire Reserve
The $300 travel credit is automatic and universally useful. The Amex Platinum's credits are more generous in total but require active management to capture.

Rewards and Earning Rates

Chase Sapphire Reserve

Earns 3x Ultimate Rewards points on travel and dining — two of the most common spending categories for premium cardholders. Everything else earns 1x. Points are worth 1.5¢ each through Chase Travel, making the effective earn rate 4.5% on travel and dining.

The 3x dining rate is a genuine differentiator — it applies to restaurants, cafes, bars, food delivery, and more worldwide.

Amex Platinum

Earns 5x Membership Rewards points on flights booked directly with airlines or through Amex Travel, and 5x on prepaid hotels through Amex Travel. Everything else earns just 1x — including dining, which is a notable gap.

The 5x on flights is exceptional for heavy flyers who book direct. But outside of airfare, the Platinum is a weak everyday earner.

Winner — Everyday Rewards
Chase Sapphire Reserve
3x on travel and dining covers most of what premium cardholders spend. The Platinum's 5x is better for flights specifically, but 1x on everything else is a significant drawback for daily use.

Airport Lounge Access

This is where the Amex Platinum wins decisively — and for frequent travelers, it's often the deciding factor.

Chase Sapphire Reserve — Priority Pass Select

The Reserve includes a Priority Pass Select membership, granting access to 1,300+ airport lounges worldwide. Priority Pass is solid — it covers a wide network globally and works in most major airports. The Reserve also recently added access to Chase Sapphire Lounges, currently open in Boston, Hong Kong, Las Vegas, New York, and more opening in 2025.

Amex Platinum — Centurion + Priority Pass

The Platinum gives you both Priority Pass AND access to Amex Centurion Lounges — widely considered the best airport lounges operated by any credit card issuer. Centurion Lounges feature chef-designed menus, full bars, spa services, and shower suites at locations in New York JFK, Los Angeles, Dallas, Las Vegas, Seattle, Miami, Philadelphia, Denver, and more.

If you fly through airports with Centurion Lounges regularly, this benefit alone can justify the card's higher fee. A typical airport meal and drinks that a Centurion Lounge replaces might cost $40-60 per visit — at 10 visits per year, that's $400-600 in real value.

Winner — Lounge Access
Amex Platinum
Centurion Lounges are genuinely exceptional and not available on any other consumer card. If you fly through Centurion Lounge airports regularly, this is the Platinum's strongest argument.

Transfer Partners

Both cards earn points in transferable currencies — one of the most valuable features in premium travel cards.

Chase Ultimate Rewards

Transfers to 14 partners including United Airlines, Southwest, Hyatt, Marriott, British Airways, Air France/KLM, Singapore Airlines, and more. The Hyatt partnership is particularly valuable — Hyatt points can be worth 2¢+ each at luxury properties, and Chase transfers 1:1.

Amex Membership Rewards

Transfers to 20+ partners including Delta, British Airways, Air France/KLM, Emirates, Lufthansa, Singapore Airlines, Hilton, and Marriott. The Delta partnership is exclusive to Amex among major US card issuers — if you're a Delta loyalist, this alone can tip the scales.

Winner — Transfer Partners
Amex Platinum (slight edge)
More partners including Delta (exclusive to Amex), Emirates, and Lufthansa. Chase wins on the Hyatt partnership. Both are excellent — the better option depends on which airlines and hotels you use most.

Who Should Get Each Card

Get the Chase Sapphire Reserve if:

  • You spend heavily on dining and want strong everyday rewards
  • You want a simple, automatic travel credit with no enrollment games
  • You rent cars frequently and want primary coverage
  • You're building a Chase ecosystem (pairs well with Freedom Unlimited and Ink cards)
  • You want higher point value when redeeming through a travel portal (1.5¢ vs 1¢)
  • You fly United, Southwest, or Hyatt is your preferred hotel brand

Get the Amex Platinum if:

  • You fly frequently and regularly pass through Centurion Lounge airports
  • You're loyal to Delta Airlines (exclusive Amex transfer partner)
  • You book premium international flights and want 5x on airfare
  • You stay at luxury hotels and will use the Fine Hotels & Resorts credit
  • You want hotel elite status at both Hilton and Marriott without earning stays
  • You're disciplined about maximizing monthly credits

Can you have both? Yes — and some serious travel hackers do. The Reserve handles dining rewards and everyday spending while the Platinum covers flight purchases (5x) and lounge access. But for most people, one card is enough.

Our Pick

For most people, the Chase Sapphire Reserve is the better card. The $300 travel credit is effortless, the 3x on dining covers what most Americans actually spend on, the 1.5¢ point value makes redemptions more valuable, and primary rental car insurance is a genuinely useful perk that saves money every time you rent.

The Amex Platinum is the right choice if Centurion Lounges are a major part of your travel life or if you're a dedicated Delta flyer. It's also worth considering if you fly enough internationally to earn significant points at the 5x rate on airfare.
